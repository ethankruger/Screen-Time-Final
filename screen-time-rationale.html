Rationale: Screen Time and Your Child's Brain

By Ethan Kruger
Published on December 14, 2025
In This Article
  • Project Overview and Publication Choice
  • Audience Analysis and Goals
  • Tone and Rhetorical Approach
  • Organizational Structure and Research Integration
  • Why This Project Matters

Project Overview and Publication Choice

For my multimodal project, I chose to do a magazine article, and I designed my magazine for Parents Magazine. My topic is how early exposure to digital media (TV, tablets, smartphones, and video games) affects children’s executive functions, such as attention, impulse control, memory, and emotional regulation. I felt that my topic fit the mission statement of Parents Magazine perfectly, which is “Parents is dedicated to supporting caregivers through every stage of the parenting journey by providing trustworthy, inclusive, expert-backed information and a supportive community to help raise confident, compassionate children”. So, with the information in my article, I want parents to make informed decisions based on which devices and content types harm which executive functions.

Audience Analysis and Goals

My audience for my article would be parents of preschool to middle school aged kids. I focused the article on the parents who are going to be constantly making decisions about what is safe media during the critical window of child development, when attention, impulse control, memory, and emotional regulation are sensitive. My specific audience might be that parent who knows that too much screen time is bad, but not why, or lacks that understanding of how TV affects memory or why smartphones and fast media impact impulse control. What I need parents and my audience to see and gather from my article is the biological evidence, the MRI scans that show lower white matter, not just the behavioral changes we know are there. I want parents to have an actual image in their imagination of what that white matter looks like. It’s a crosswalk for impulse control, emotional regulation, and brain communication, and when white matter is disrupted during development, it can be detrimental. I hope to give some sort of understanding that the type of media matters more than minutes of screen time, because YouTube on a smartphone affects impulse control differently than educational apps on a tablet.

Tone and Rhetorical Approach

Some of the choices I made for my article, involving my tone and my delivery, were to be empowering while not being judgmental. I wanted to avoid language that a parent would read and say to themselves, “Am I a bad parent for allowing background TV?” It helps parents to be the decision makers based on real scientific evidence brought to them by someone who has researched it. I hoped to add relatability, noting that everyone knows TikTok and YouTube are dopamine draining platforms, and it’s not a niche problem. I think this helps to build trust and credibility in my writing. The nonjudgmental approach is what I wanted to do, make it seem like it’s a choice the parent should take, not one they are forced to take by guilt.

Organizational Structure and Research Integration

The structure of my article was simple, but effective in my mind. I first wanted to lay out definitions for attention, impulse control, memory, and emotional regulation with examples of each. Then I moved into the problems like “How Screen Time Affects the Brain” and the “Background TV Problem”, which allowed me to apply those definitions I purposely put at the start of the article. Then I moved more into context with “Age-Appropriate Guidelines”, hitting parents with time frames of specific ages of when children are vulnerable, and making the problem come to life in their heads. Lastly, I ended with solutions, not “you are going to do this,” but suggestions on how things can be done better. The section “What Parents Can Do Now” provides the device specific steps and fixes after explaining how each affects specific executive functions. To structure my research, I incorporated my 8 sources throughout the article in easy ways to understand the information and keep the article going, and not letting the information chop up the article. For example, instead of saying “reduced white matter affects the prefrontal cortex,” I explained this by saying “the brain’s wiring system”. It makes it easier to understand, but still mentions the white matter. I named researchers like Dr. Hutton, Nemanja Lakicevic, and Ke Xu to build credibility in my article, and a reason to follow my information.

Why This Project Matters

The reason this topic matters is clear from my article, it matters because more and more each generation is getting easier access to technology and fast paced media. Children are getting access to this during sensitive times in development, when executive functions are vulnerable to exploitation. My topic matters because there are parents who know screens are bad in high volume, but not why, they may not know background TV affects memory, or how smartphone social media and fast media differ from educational apps when it comes to impulse control. So with my article, every citation of evidence and recommendation was to allow parents to make informed decisions on how to structure their children’s development when it comes to screens and media.
