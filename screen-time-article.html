Screen Time and Your Child's Brain: What Every Parent Should Know About Executive Function Development

By Ethan Kruger
Published on December 14, 2025
In This Article
  • Understanding Executive Functions
  • How Screen Time Affects the Brain
  • The Background TV Problem
  • Fast-Paced Media and Attention
  • Age-Appropriate Guidelines
  • What Parents Can Do Now

Key Takeaways

  • Executive functions—working memory, inhibitory control, and cognitive flexibility—are foundational for learning, emotional regulation, and healthy risk-taking.
  • Early exposure to fast-paced media and tides of digital screens can disrupt neural circuits that support sustained attention.
  • Background TV degrades deep thinking, even if children aren’t watching it directly.
  • Smart, age-appropriate limits and mindful co-viewing can safeguard executive function development.

Understanding Executive Functions

To understand how executive functions in children are affected, we must start by knowing what they are. Based on scientific research, there are three core executive functions, inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility.

Inhibitory control is the ability to control impulses and manage emotional responses, so just based on the definition, we can see why this is important in children. Working memory is the ability to hold information in your mind while working through a problem. A quick example is when you ask your child to put away their shoes, then wash their hands, and then finally come to dinner. This tests their working memory.

Cognitive flexibility is behavioral monitoring and checking personal behavior for errors and adjusting actions when something isn’t working. For example, this is the skill that allows children to switch strategies during a puzzle or adjust their behavior after a mistake they have made.

So, when a child grabs a toy from another child or struggles to wait their turn, they’re not being difficult, they are developing brain functions and skills, which we call executive functions. Understanding what these functions are is the first step to protecting children’s brains, because new research shows that what happens on screens can reshape how these skills develop.

How Screen Time Affects the Brain

There is a lot of research that shows screen time and its effect on a child’s behavior, but it doesn’t stop there, it is even biological. In a 2020 study, pediatric radiologist Dr. John Hutton did MRI scans on preschoolers and used the data to uncover that those children with higher screen exposure showed reduced white matter in key brain regions.

Okay, cool discovery, but what does this mean? You can think of white matter as the brain's wiring. It helps to connect different regions of the brain so they can communicate, and it just so happens that these pathways are essential for memory, attention, and inhibitory control skills.

Structural brain changes are just part of it, though, according to psychologist David Bredehoft, prolonged screen time for children also overloads the brain’s reward system through dopamine. In today’s age of technology, we hear about dopamine and what platforms like TikTok do to dopamine in our brains, but it is especially important in a developing brain because overstimulation of dopamine in a child can rewire the brain’s tolerance for activities that require sustained attention and effort.

So, we know some real effects, but let’s talk specifics. Dr. Hutton’s research shows that smartphone media is a particularly big problem. His research shows that smartphone media decreased white matter in parts of the brain responsible for language processing. He also highlights that platforms like YouTube, TikTok, and even children’s cartoons that are fast-paced affect attention skills.

So, the result is children with poorer attention regulation and slower brain processing speeds. So, the evidence is pretty clear, what children watch and how long they watch leaves real biological changes.

The Background TV Problem

One thing that may go unchecked by parents is extra noise in the background, a show no one is watching, or the news playing while their kid is in the room trying to do something. It seems harmless, but it is not. So, what is Background TV? Background TV is described as media playing in the background while a child is completing a task or playing.

“The TV is on, but your toddler is building with Legos across the room. No harm done, right?”

Wrong. Background TV can have effects on children’s brain functions while just completing simple tasks like eating dinner, doing puzzles, or doing homework. According to the Institute of Digital Media and Child Development, this habit directly harms working memory and attention in children.

So even when children aren’t actively watching the TV, their brains are constantly trying to understand and pull towards the screen, which pieces their focus apart. This comes into direct connection with working memory. Psychologist Sumudu Mallawaarachchi’s meta-analysis found that there are, in fact, links between background TV and worse working memory performance.

Each time the TV draws even a little bit of attention from the child, it disrupts their ability to maintain focus on their task. So, when it comes to background TV, the damage done is not from the particular media, it is from the distraction of pulling your child’s brain away from activities that are building strong brain functions.

Fast-Paced Media and Attention

We talked about an unknown culprit, background TV, but let’s talk about something we all know, YouTube, TikTok, and fast-paced media. We will talk about media in two different ways, fast-paced media and interactive media. Public health researcher Nemanja Lakicevic defines both like this, passive screen use is TV, YouTube, and cartoons, where children simply watch content unfold, while interactive screen use involves activities where children engage with the content, like educational apps and video games.

These definitions challenge the idea that total screen time is what matters most, instead, it might be the type of media the child is consuming. Here is why. Fast-paced media is shown to overwhelm developing systems with the rapid screen changes, bright and super engaging colors, and constant dopamine stimulation. According to Bredehoft’s research, this fast-paced media causes immediate attention drops in young children, basically tricking their brains into always expecting excitement.

Interestingly, though, not all interactive media is harmful. Behavioral scientist Ke Xu found that strategic games can actually help to improve working memory and brain flexibility in children. But what’s the real difference? Strategy games require planning, real problem solving, and sustained focus from a kid, the exact things that can actually help executive functions develop.

Another really interesting find that Lakicevic found was that educational apps actually fall somewhere in the middle, depending on design, the pace, and engagement style is what really matters. So, there is a clear takeaway here, 30 minutes of educational content or problem-solving video games is not the same as 30 minutes of YouTube Kids or TikTok.

Age-Appropriate Guidelines

When it comes to screen exposure, age truly does play a role. The content that is manageable for a 10 year old might not be manageable for a preschool aged child. Preschool aged kids are where most research falls, and almost all of it shows the strongest negative impacts from screen exposure.

This makes sense because Best and Miller explained, executive functions and their development are basically in a waiting line right behind the prefrontal cortex to develop. Developmental scientist Caroline Fitzpatrick did research on this, her research shows that longer screen exposure doesn’t just immediately affect children, but over time, it shows worse executive functions.

Through Ke Xu’s research, it was found that the stage a child is in while developing executive functions, determines whether media supports or harms them. This explains why educational games are good for an 8 year old, while fast paced video games might affect their inhibitory control.

There is good news, Mallawaarachchi’s research emphasizes that how, when, and what kind of media children use matters a lot more than time limits parents can set. One example Mallawaarachchi brings up is called “Co-Viewing”, watching alongside your child, he says this can moderate some of the side effects.

So, the goal shouldn’t be to eliminate all screens, but it should be to understand which types pose the greatest risk to a developing child. So, understanding that the critical development window doesn’t just affect the 3 year old, it affects that future 8 year old, and eventual 18 year old as well.

What Parents Can Do Now

So, I have given you a lot of information today, but what can you, as a parent, do now?

Action #1: Turn off background TV completely

This super, super simple action might be one of the most powerful things you can do to protect your child’s working memory during development. So, if you’re not actively watching a show, nor is your child, turn it off. This background noise is chipping away at their attention span and affecting their ability to focus on real activities.

Action #2: Limit Fast-Paced Content

Limit YouTube, TikTok, and faced paced TV show content. These platforms and shows are designed with rapid scenes to keep children drawn to them. And through Lakicevic’s research, that is linked to weakened inhibitory control. It’s not about eliminating all of it but rather choosing content that doesn’t overstimulate.

Action #3: Choose Quality Over Quantity

Focus on the type of media, 30 minutes of slow paces media that educates is not the same as 30 minutes of TikTok or video games. For older children, consider implementing strategy-based video games, backed by Xu’s research, which can help with working memory and cognitive flexibility.

Action #4: Prioritize Real World Activities

Build executive functions through puzzles, board games, outside play, and activities that use attention and focus on children. Remembering that Best and Miller’s research shows that executive functions are learned and developed through practice, so when children do activities that use inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility, they are creating white matter pathways.

In the end, the science is there, but given that, so is the solution. Make informed choices about the media your child sees and make choices that will create healthier habits for your child and the development of their executive functions.
